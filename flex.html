FLEX (Thesis)

Summary

I started this solo project by looking into the occurrence of injuries in musicians and trying to identify trends that stood out that might point towards an underlying cause.

I started my search in the Music Library at Northwestern University and soon started speaking to experts in the field, Professors of music, Physiotherapists, sports medicine doctors,  performance therapy doctors at the RIC, and finally with students from the Bienen school of Music.

My findings from the interviews with experts as well as the students revealed that while most experts recommended some form of physical training for musicians, (such as is present in the culture of athleticism) most students of musical performance did not abide by this recommendation. In fact, most of the students who did stretch, exercise the muscles and joints that they used while playing their instruments for hours on end had either been injured previously, or had an athletic background as well.

With these findings in hand, I refined my project statement to "How Might we motivate students of musical performance to take prophylactic action against Repetitive motion injuries"

I then used this direction to guide my ideation and brainstorming , quickly dismissing some concepts based on timeframe, feasibility, or simply because they were too far-fetched to work.

 

One idea that stuck was the concept of a platform that one could use to perform basic stretches. It would be mechanically passive but have sensors to measure the force input data and use it in some form of data visualization.

I made a quick and dirty prototype to gain feedback from users as well as experts. I chose the wrist extension stretch because it is mechanically simple and small enough to prototype and move around as compared to some other types of stretches

Having received positive feedback on the idea, I moved forward with bringing it to life.

 

I proceeded to do this by defining in great detail what the product and interaction would look like, and how it would function. One of the requirements I had was to measure force, a function that the Nintendo Wii balance board could perform with great accuracy and repeatability.

I then proceeded to design and build the circuit for sensing the voltage across the load cells using the Arduino MCU and a breadboard

Once the electronics were in place and working, I build the platform upon which the board would sit. It had to be hinged because the angle that forms between the wrist and the arm while engaging with the device varies by the height of the user. a simple fix was to create a mechanism that would adjust itself to provide the optimal angle for stretching one's wrist

I used a 150 lb 4" linear actuator controlled with an H bridge to tilt the board. The wooden stops exist to prevent the mechanism from ripping the screws out of the board at its lowest point.

 

The limit switch exists to inform the MCU when the board is at its lowest position

Alongside the development of the physical prototype, I started to create a graphical representation of the 'game' in the form of a ball (coded in processing). The position of the ball was controlled by the input on the load cell, which was read by the ADC converter on the Arduino and communicated to the processing code via serial port.

Because there are multiple inputs (4 load cells, although for simplicity I was using just two) The information had to be sent as a string and then split and parsed into the correct values for each input.

 

Here's an image of the first functioning prototype with the display.

Once the main interaction was set, I took it to my users to garner feedback. I realized that I had to create the system that allowed it to be used without any explanation, an app of sorts. I started laying out the possible screens and the flowchart of possible interactions. Then I created the App screens on illustrator and exported them to processing as simple JPEGs. Navigation was achieved through two large physical buttons, as signified by the blue and white circles

After one more round of iteration on the interface, graphics and mild adjustments to the electronics to increase the robustness of the system, I took it out one last time for testing. The highlights of which can be seen in the video below
