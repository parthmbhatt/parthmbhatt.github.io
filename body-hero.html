Body Hero

Summary

We wanted to create an experience for our users that allowed them to interact with music in a new and exciting way.

 

With this objective in mind we brainstormed several modes and methods of interaction with music, and we ideated on new and interesting ways in which we could bring the experience to life.

We finally filtered out the other ideas based on feasibility, and effectiveness, and decided to pursue BodyHero.

 

It would work similarly to guitar hero, the user would receive cues based on the music being played, but instead of playing it on an instrument, they'd play it on themselves.

We tapped into the common action of tapping along with a beat while listening to music and mapped it onto the buttons that would act as the controls, then when the music (Eye of the Tiger, of course) played, they'd have to play along with it.

The buttons had haptic motors embedded in their housings, which served to deliver the cues for the music.

We used an array containing 3 columns (for the three buttons) to 'record' the beat of the song. We used this method because we didn't have the time to figure out how to make the MIDI library work.

 

The array breaks down the song 'Eye of the Tiger' into 8th notes (which makes every row signifies 1/8th of a single count in the song)

 

Since the accents in the song are largely 1/4 notes, that gave us a resolution of two rows of the array for every accent.

The MCU would read the array, and send the appropriate signal to the vibration motor, and then read for the buttons being pressed. If the user was performing well, the volume would go up, if not, it would drop.
