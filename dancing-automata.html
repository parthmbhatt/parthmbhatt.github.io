Dancing Automata

I created a pair of Dancing automata as a gift for a friend

Project Definition

I wanted to make something nice for a friend of mine who I'd learned to dance with, and I thought that an Automaton or even an arbitrated physical representation of dance might make for a thoughtful gift as well as an interesting project.

 

I started out with some rough Ideas, some half decent, others not so much

Ideation

I ended up with three prominent ideas. One was a board with silhouettes of footsteps moving in different dance patterns. the lights would work similar to how crosswalk signals do, with two different shapes with two colours superimposed over one another. I could have used an LED or LCD display but that didn't quite seem as novel to me

Another Idea was to make a track and have figurines move across the track, with the female figurine doing twirls as it moved.

 

I discarded this idea primarily because of how difficult it seemed to get the figurines to move back and forth without some sort of sophisticated motion system involving linear bearings

I settled on the third concept, which would be a simple rotary system that would represent a perpetual twirl using just gears. I went with this because of it's simplicity

Mechanism Design

Before moving to CAD I mapped out the mechanism and the essential elements such as the housing, the stationary and rotating parts, the gear ratio that I wanted (I didn't want it to seem like the female figurine was being spun too fast)

CAD for Laser Cutting

I modeled the gears using the Toolbox in SolidWorks , and then added the figures and their platforms.

 

For the first version of the machine I only had access to a laser cutter, so I had to create the housing by stacking rings of acrylic (though in retrospect I should have just made a finger-jointed box) and I used a geared down motor that I found lying around.

the figurines are also stacked acrylic. I misjudged the height of the motor so I had to throw in an additional platform (of Lego) under it to house the battery and switch

CAD for 3D printing

For the second iteration of the machine, I only had access to an FDM 3D printer (Ultimaker 2+), and so had to redo the design to make it compatible for 3D printing. I also made some improvements to the figurines that would increase the strength, and reduced the material required to build the base.

 

For this version I decided to use a servo motor instead of a regular DC motor because it's dimensions would be easily available.

Modifying a servo

Once I had the dimensions of the servo, I had to modify it to be able to rotate continuously rather than just 180Â°. The mechanism of a servo motor is basically controlled by a potentiometer and a physical stop in one of the gears. To enable continuous motion, I opened the motor, and broke the physical stops inside it, turning it into an ordinary geared DC motor

I resolved to use just one battery this time to reduce the rotational speed of the motor(There are more elegant ways of doing this but they're not quite as simple) and to do this, I modified a battery compartment by soldering the wire to the other end of the first cell's terminal.

I had designed the holes to be size M6, and I found corresponding pins in the machine Shop to act as shafts. The connector arm has a recess the size of the servo horn and is super-glued onto it.

Final Assembly

I mounted the final assembled device on a piece of black acrylic (I had to raise the height of the main housing because of a discrepancy in the documented dimensions of the motor). The motor is secured to the acrylic by super-gluing two brackets to the base, and the battery compartment is removable and held in place by magnets.
